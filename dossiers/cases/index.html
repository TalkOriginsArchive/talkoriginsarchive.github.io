Dossiers: Cases

Legal Case Dossiers: Reader's Primer

Route: /dossiers/cases/ · TalkOrigins feature page.

This page is a reader's guide to the legal case dossiers in the TalkOrigins Archive. It explains the parts of a United States court record most useful for following creation-science, intelligent-design, and public-school science cases.

Legal records are not organized like essays. A useful path is often: identify the challenged rule, read the decision for the court's reasoning, then use testimony, depositions, and exhibits to understand how the factual record was built.

Legal Proceedings: Quick Primer

Civil, Not Criminal

Creation-science and intelligent-design school cases are usually civil lawsuits. The court is deciding whether a law or school-board action is lawful, not whether someone committed a crime.

Bench Trial or Jury Trial

A bench trial is decided by a judge. McLean and Kitzmiller were bench trials, which is why the written findings explain both the facts and the legal reasoning in detail.

Law and Equity

Historically, common-law courts awarded legal remedies such as damages, while courts of equity could order or prevent actions. Modern federal courts can handle both kinds of remedy, but older records may still use that vocabulary.

Trial, Appeal, and Precedent

District courts try facts and produce trial records. Appellate courts review legal rulings. Supreme Court decisions control more broadly than district-court decisions.

Historical Case Context

The Scopes Trial was a state criminal prosecution over teaching evolution. McLean, Edwards, and Kitzmiller were civil challenges to government policy about science education.

First Amendment Question

In these education cases, the key constitutional issue is usually the Establishment Clause: whether government action promotes or endorses religion in public-school science instruction.

Document Types

A statute says what the legislature enacted; a complaint starts a case; depositions are sworn pretrial testimony; trial transcripts record courtroom testimony; a decision states what the court found and ordered.

How to Read a Case Dossier

  1. Start with the case summary. It tells you what public action was challenged and why the record matters.
  2. Read the decision or opinion next. The court's decision is the best map to the legal issues and factual findings.
  3. Use statutes, testimony, and depositions as supporting record. These documents show what the court was asked to evaluate.
  4. Compare related cases carefully. A district-court case, a Supreme Court case, and a later school-board case may address similar issues but carry different legal weight.
