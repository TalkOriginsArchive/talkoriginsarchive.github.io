Title: Dark peppered moths in pre-industrial times
Keywords: biston betularia, carbonifera

Claim:

The dark form of peppered moth has always existed in the peppered moth
population.  Peppered moth evolution merely shows changes in relative
numbers of the different varieties.  Nothing new originated.

Source:

Response:

1. In the absence of soot to darken the trees, the dark form of peppered
   moths is selected against at a rate that would cause its extinction
   in a few decades.

2. There is no evidence that any of the dark moths were seen before 1848,
   despite the fact that naturalists of the time would almost certainly
   have taken notice of any they saw.  John Ray (1710) and Linnaeus (1758)
   describe only the light variety.  Ford (date?, 329) noted a single
   specimen of the dark peppered moth in the William Jones collection in
   the Department of Entomology at Oxford University, caught sometime
   before 1811, about 30 years after the Industrial Revolution came to
   England.  Kettlewell and R. C. Steward examined the lepidoptera in the
   British National Museum and Steward looked in the Welsh National Museum
   looking for the earliest specimens and found none from earlier than the
   mid-1800s (Steward 1977).

Of course there were people looking for black moths then.  The 17th and
18th centuries were the period of the scientific revolution, when Europe
experienced an explosion of interest in all sorts of scientific activities,
not the least of which was natural history.  People like John Ray
and Carl von Linn (aka Linnaeus), to name just two of the most prominent
naturalists of the time, were scouring all over the place not just for black
moths, but for moths of _any_ colour, or for any other bugs, plants or animals
they could lay their hands on.   Since there is not a shred of evidence that
anyone captured, or even observed, a black _peppered_ moth before the
industrial revolution, it is of course highly unlikely that any naturalists
of the time would have _specifically_ searched for such a thing.  Why should
they, if they weren't even aware of its existence?  

Nevertheless, there is little doubt that any of the more knowledgeable
naturalists of the time (such as Ray or Linnaeus, for instance), would have
found such a specimen extremely interesting, and would hardly have failed to
mention it somewhere in their writings on the peppered moth if they had ever
come across an instance.  Indeed, in his book _The Evolution of Melanism_
(p.131), Kettlewell notes that once the existence of the melanic varieties
became known in England in the mid 19th century they were much sought
after by collectors.  So if their existence had been known to any knowledgeable
collectors before then it seems reasonable to expect that there would be
some record of it.

Guess what.  Ray's _History of Insects_ of 1710 contains a description of
the normal light peppered moth but none of any dark varieties.  Likewise, the
only variety of peppered moth desribed in the historic 10th edition of
Linnaeus's _Systema Naturae_ of 1758 (as well as all subsequent editions) is
the normal light variety.  Likewise, all other illustrations or descriptions of
peppered moths which have so far come to light from the entomological
literature of the 17th and 18th centuries have been of the light variety.  It
would appear that there is no trace of the existence of any dark variety to be
found anywhere in this literature.

In the 4th edition of his _Ecological Genetics_ (p.329), E.B. Ford noted
that the William Jones collection in the Department of Entomology at
Oxford University contains a single specimen of the dark peppered moth that
was caught at an unknown location some time before 1811.  This is by
far the earliest specimen of dark moth that has so far come to light
from any collection anywhere.  Note that by 1811 the industrial revolution
had been under way in England for at least 30 years.

Before Ford pointed out this specimen, Kettlewell had already examined the
National Collection of British Lepidoptera in the British Museum.  He
found no dark peppered moths dating to any earlier than the mid 19th century.
Subsequently, R.C.Steward (_Ecological Entomology_, 2 (1977), p.232) examined
this collection, as well as that in the Welsh National Museum and the
Hope Department in Oxford, similarly without finding any earlier dark
peppered moths.  Steward's investigation seems to have been carried out
_after_ Ford had already pointed out the existence of a specimen pre-dating
1811, but he appears to have been unaware of it.

See above.  Unfortunately, details given in text-book accounts of the 18th
and 19th century history of the peppered moth are not always reliable.
The usual date given for the earliest sighting is 1848. The only evidence
I have ever seen cited for this is a letter from one R.S.Edelston to the
1864-65 issue of _The Entomologist_ (vol 2, p.150).  I have a photocopy
of this, which reads as follows, in its entirety:

  _Amphydasis betularia_. --- Some sixteen years ago the "negro" aberration
  of this common species was almost unknown; more recently it has been
  had by several parties.  Last year I obtained the eggs of a female of
  the common form, which had been _in cop._ with a "negro" male : the
  larvae I fed on willow, and this year had some remarkably pretty
  aberrations, the connecting-link between the "negro" and the usual
  form, but far before either as regards beauty : I placed some of the
  virgin females in my garden, in order to attract the males, and was not
  a little surprised to find that most of the visitors were the "negro"
  aberration :  If this goes on for a few years the original type of
  A. betularia will be extinct in this locality.
