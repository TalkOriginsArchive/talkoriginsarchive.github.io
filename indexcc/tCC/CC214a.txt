BriefClaim: There are gaps between reptile-bird.
Title: Transitional Birds
Keywords: transitional, bird, Archaeopteryx, dinosaur.

Claim:

There are no transitional fossils between reptiles and birds.

Source:

LifeHow, 75.

Response:

1. Intermediates between theropod dinosaurs and modern birds:

   - _Sinosauropteryx prima_ - a dinosaur covered with primitive feathers,
     but structurally similar to unfeathered dinosaurs _Ornitholestes_ and
     _Compsognathus_ [Pei-ji et al, 1998; Currie & Chen, 2001].  Another
     undescribed dinosaur is also covered with primitive feathers [Hwang
     et al, in press].
   - _Ornithomimosaurs_, therizinosaurs and oviraptorosaurs - Feathers are
     known from the therizinosaur _Beipiaosaurus_ [Xu et al., 1999; Xu et
     al., 2003b] and the oviraptorosaur _Caudipteryx_ [Ji et al., 1998;
     Zhou and Wang, 2000; Zhou et al., 2000].  _Caudipteryx_ had a body
     covering of tufted feathers and, on wings and tail, modern feathers
     with a central rachis [Padian et al., 2001].  Several other bird-like
     characters appear in these taxa, including unserrated teeth,
     highly pneumatized skulls and vertebrae, and elongate wings.
     Oviraptorids also had birdlike eggs and brooding habits [Clark et
     al., 1999].
   - Deinonychosaurs (troodontids and dromaeosaurs) - the closest known
     dinosaurs to birds.  _Sinovenator_, the most primitive troodontid, is
     especially close to _Archaeopteryx_ in morphology [Xu et al., 2002a].
     _Byronosaurus_, another troodontid, has teeth nearly identical to
     birds [Makovicky et al., 2003].  _Microraptor_, the most primitive
     dromaeosaur, is also the most birdlike [Hwang et al., 2002];
     specimens have been found with undisputed feathers on their wings,
     legs and tail [Czerkas et al., 2002; Xu et al., 2003a].
     _Sinornithosaurus_ also was covered with a variety of feathers and
     had a skull and postcranium more birdlike than later dromaeosaurs [Xu
     et al, 1999, 2001; Xu and Wu, 2001; Ji et al, 2001].  Other dinosaurs
     in this group have numerous points of birdlike anatomy, too [e.g.,
     Norell & Makovicky, 1997, 1999; Gishlick, 2001].
   - Scansoriopterygids, _Protarchaeopteryx_, alvarezsaurids,
     _Yixianosaurus_ and _Avimimus_ - These are birdlike dinosaurs of
     uncertain placement, each potentially closer to birds than
     deinonychosaurs.  _Protarchaeopteryx_, for example, has undisputed
     tail feathers, uncompressed teeth and an elongate manus (hand/wing)
     [Padian et al., 2001; Ji et al., 1998].  See also scansoriopterygids
     [Czerkas and Yuan, 2002; Zhang et al., 2002], alvarezsaurids
     [Schweitzer et al., 1999; Chiappe et al., 2002], _Yixianosaurus_ [Xu
     and Wang, 2003], and _Avimimus_ [Vickers-Rich et al., 2002].
   - <CC214.1.1>_Archaeopteryx_> - This genus is actually less birdlike
     in some ways than some genera mentioned above [Paul, 2002; Maryanska
     et al., 2002; Lu et al., 2002].
   - _Shenzhouraptor_ [Zhou and Zhang, 2002; Ji et al., 2003], _Rahonavis_
     [Forster et al., 1998], _Yandangornis_ and _Jixiangornis_ [Ji et al.,
     2002] - All these birds are slightly more advanced than
     _Archaeopteryx_, especially in characters of the vertebrae, sternum,
     and wing bones.
   - _Sapeornis_ [Zhou and Zhang, 2003], _Omnivoropteryx_, and
     confuciusornithids (e.g. _Confuciusornis_ and _Changchengornis_)
     [Chiappe et al., 1999] - The first birds to possess large pygostyles
     (bone formed from fused tail vertebrae).  Other derived characters
     include no dorsal hyposphenes or hypantra, seven sacral vertebrae, a
     sternal keel on some specimens, and a reversed hallux.
   - Enantiornithines, including many well known primitive birds such as
     _Sinornis_ [Sereno & Rao, 1992; Sereno et al., 2002], _Iberomesornis_
     [Sereno, 2000], _Eoalulavis_ [Sanz et al., 2002] and _Gobipteryx_
     [Chiappe et al., 2001] and the primitive _Protopteryx_ [Zhang and
     Zhou, 2000] and _Longipteryx_ [Zhang et al, 2001].  _Jibeinia_ [Hou,
     1997] was not described as an enantiornithine, but appears to be
     closely related.  Birdlike features that appeared in enantiornithines
     include an incomplete postorbital bar, twelve or less dorsal
     vertebrae, eight or more sacral vertebrae (not in _Protopteryx_), a
     narrow V-shaped furcula, coracoid elongated to the level of recent
     birds, distally pointed scapula, mobile scapulocoracoid joint,
     metacarpal I fused to rest of metacarpus, less than four phalanges on
     manual digit III, and an alula.
   - _Patagopteryx_, _Apsaravis_ and yanornithids [Chiappe, 2002; Clarke and
     Norell, 2002; Zhou and Zhang, 2001].
   - _Hesperornithines_, _Ichthyornis_, _Gansus_ and _Limenavis_ -
     birds that are almost as advanced as modern species.

2. Bird feathers have a transitional form on the dromaeosaurid dinosaur
   _Sinornithosaurus millenii_, which has filamentous integumental appendages
   which are compound structures composed of multiple filaments and which
   show two types of branching structures characteristic of feathers [Xu
   et al, 2001].
