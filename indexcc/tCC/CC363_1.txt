Title: Fossil fish in varves
Keywords: varve, taphonomy, gradualism

Claim:

Geologists claim that varves formed gradually, just one thin layer per
year.  But varve formations contain abundant fossil fish.  In modern
lakes, fish decay in a few days, even in oxygen-poor conditions and
protected from scavengers.  The fish must have been buried suddenly to be
preserved as fossils.

Source:

Garner, Paul. 1997.  Green River blues.  _Creation_ 19(3): 18-19.
 http://www.answersingenesis.org/creation/v19/i3/greenriver.asp

Response:

1. The fish to which Garner refers are found in only one part of the Green
   River Formation, in the deepest part of the ancient lake Gosiute
   furthest from the shore.  The anoxic conditions that deep could have
   preserved the fish until they were buried.

   Fossilization depends on a number of conditions,
   including oxygen, salinity, water depth, temperature, chemical
   composition of the sediments, and many other factors.  One can even
   determine much about the lake environment from the condition of the
   fossils (Barton and Wilson 2005).

2. There are numerous indications that the fossil fish and the Green River
   Formation generally could not have been deposited rapidly.
    - The fish fossils are intact.  If they had been transported even a
      short distance, they would have been at least partly
      disarticulated.
    - The catfish fossils are confined to a relatively small area.
    - Abundant fish coprolites (fossil dung) appear with the fish.  They
      would have required a long time to accumulate.
    - There are surface features, including tracks and mud cracks,
      fossilized at the shore of the lake.
    - There are built-up structures, up to nine meters tall, made of
      fossilized caddisfly larval cases along what was the northern shore
      of Lake Gosiute.  It would have taken many years for the caddisflies
      to build up such an accumulation.
