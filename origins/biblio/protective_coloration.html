Protective coloration

Bibliography index · /origins/biblio/protective_coloration.html

Protective coloration refers to the use of coloration for concealment or warning in animals, a concept historically debated by figures like Abbott H. Thayer. This bibliography documents the scientific and historical literature surrounding these ideas, including their development in evolutionary biology and their application in military camouflage. It serves as a resource for understanding the origins of camouflage theory and its intersection with natural history.

10. Behrens, Roy R., 1988, The Theories of Abbott H. Thayer: Father of Camouflage: Leonardo.

DOI: 10.2307/1578658

Abstract

Abbott H. Thayer (1849–1921), a prominent U.S. painter, was one of the first to describe in detail the function of protective coloration in nature. In military history, he frequently is credited with the first military applications of countershading and disruptive patterning, so that he was known among friends as ‘the father of camouflage’. This essay is a brief review of Thayer’s lifelong involvement in the study of both natural and military camouflage.

20. Behrens, R., 2016, Seeing through Camouflage: Abbott Thayer, Background-Picturing and the Use of Cutout Silhouettes: Leonardo: v. 51, no. 1: p. 40-46.

DOI: 10.1162/LEON_a_01337 Source

Abstract

In the first decade of the twentieth century, while researching protective coloration in nature, artist-naturalist Abbott H. Thayer (1849–1921), working with his son, Gerald H. Thayer (1883–1939), hypothesized a kind of camouflage that he called “background-picturing.” It was his contention that, in many animals, the patterns on their bodies make it seem as if one could “see through” them, as if they were transparent. This essay revisits that concept, Thayer’s descriptions and demonstrations of it, and compares it to current computer-based practices of replacing gaps in images with “content-aware” digital patches.
