TalkOrigins Bibliography Topic

Pseudoscience

Bibliography index · /origins/biblio/pseudoscience.html

Pseudoscience refers to claims or practices that falsely present themselves as scientific, often lacking empirical evidence or rigorous methodology. This bibliography examines such phenomena within the context of the creation/evolution controversy, focusing on "origin science" and other fringe theories that misrepresent deep time or biological origins. It includes critical analyses of historical and contemporary examples, such as astrology, ESP, and creationist arguments, to distinguish genuine scientific inquiry from deceptive or unsupported assertions.

31. Coles, John, 2002, Chariots of the Gods? Landscape and Imagery at Frännarp, Sweden: Proceedings of the Prehistoric Society: v. 68: p. 215-246.

DOI: 10.1017/s0079497x00001511

Abstract

The rock carving at Frännarp has been known for a century and has been the subject of many reports, always concentrating upon the detailed representation of Bronze Age carts. A new recording of the site has revealed the existence of many faint carvings, including small carts, many discs, and also a number of paired animals for the well-known cart designs. Field surveys around the site show a dense concentration of burial and other monuments in the immediate area. The Frännarp site must have served as a special focus here and also for a wider cultural landscape, and a land-based route is identified which may have aided access from further afield.

36. Stea, Jonathan N., 2024, The Evolution and Seduction of Pseudoscience: Mind the Science: p. 7-23.

DOI: 10.1093/oso/9780197748817.003.0002

Abstract

This chapter details the evolution and seduction of mental health–related pseudoscience. It begins by providing historical and modern-day examples of snake oil, couching them in the context of the author’s personal and clinical experiences. It illustrates the nature of pseudoscience, delineating between unequivocal cases (e.g., naturopathy, energy healing) and controversial ones that are more scientifically plausible but still plagued by some pseudoscientific features and low-quality evidence to support their use (e.g., mindfulness meditation, psychedelics). It goes on to show the reasons that we find pseudoscience attractive, foreshadowing the commercialization of pseudoscience in the wellness and alternative medicine industries. The chapter closes with real examples about the various ways that pseudoscience is harmful to our mental health.
