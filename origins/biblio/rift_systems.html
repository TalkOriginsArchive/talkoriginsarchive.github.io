TalkOrigins Bibliography Topic

Rift systems

Bibliography index · /origins/biblio/rift_systems.html

Rift systems are geological features where the Earth's crust is being pulled apart, often leading to the formation of unique mineral deposits. This bibliography collects sources on rift-related metalliferous deposits, focusing on their geological genesis and stratigraphic context. These citations provide evidence for deep time processes and plate tectonics, relevant to earth history and scientific understanding of crustal formation.

1. Ochsenius, Carl, 1884, V.—Metalliferous Deposits: Geological Magazine: v. 1, no. 7: p. 310-318.

DOI: 10.1017/s0016756800005276

Abstract

The origin of metalliferous deposits has long been a subject of discussion. Professor Joseph Le Conte, however, seems to have arrived at a very decided opinion on this question, for in a contribution to the “American Journal of Science” (3rd series, xxvi. p. 1—19, July, 1883), after referring to Sulphur Banks and Steamboat Springs in California, he says: “Thus then subterranean waters of any kind, but especially alkaline, at any temperature, but mostly hot, circulating in any direction, but mainly upcoming, and in any kind of waterway, but mainly in open fissures, by deposit, form metalliferous veins.”

4. Kanasewich, E. R., 1968, Precambrian Rift: Genesis of Strata-Bound Ore Deposits: Science: v. 161, no. 3845: p. 1002-1005.

DOI: 10.1126/science.161.3845.1002

Abstract

Study of deep seismic reflections has detected a Precambrian rift valley below flat-lying sediments in southern Alberta. The anomalous magnetic and gravity trends show that the rift is continuous across Alberta and British Columbia (through the Kimberley lead-zinc field) and possibly the Coeur d'Alene mining district of Idaho. There is evidence that these ore bodies were deposited in a Precambrian rift under conditions similar to those prevailing in the hot-brine areas of the modern Red Sea.
