Volcanism

Bibliography index · /origins/biblio/volcanism.html

Volcanism encompasses the geological processes and historical eruptions that shape Earth's surface and influence climate. This bibliography includes scientific studies on events like Mount St. Helens and Krakatau, alongside creationist arguments linking volcanic activity to biblical narratives. These sources address deep time, earth history, and the scientific method within the creation/evolution controversy.

12. Hobbs, Peter V. and Radke, Lawrence F. and Eltgroth, Mark W. and Hegg, Dean A., 1981, Airborne Studies of the Emissions from the Volcanic Eruptions of Mount St. Helens: Science: v. 211, no. 4484: p. 816-818.

DOI: 10.1126/science.211.4484.816

Abstract

The concentrations of particles less than 10 micrometers in diameter in the ash emissions from Mount St. Helens have been more than 1000 times greater than those in the ambient air. Mass loadings of particles less than 2 micrometers in diameter were generally several hundred micrograms per cubic meter. In the ash clouds, produced by the large eruption on 18 May 1980, the concentrations of several trace gases generally were low. In other emissions, significant, but variable, concentrations of sulfur gases were measured. The 18 May eruption produced nuées ardentes, lightning flashes, and volcanic hail.

DOI: 10.1126/science.212.4501.1383

Abstract

An energy-balance numerical climate model was used to simulate the effects of the Mount St. Helens volcanic eruption of 18 May 1980. The resulting surface temperature depression is a maximum of 0.1°C in the winter in the polar region, but is an order of magnitude smaller than the observed natural variability from other effects and will therefore be undetectable.
